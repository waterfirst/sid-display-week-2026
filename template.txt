Dual View Display Technology for Enhanced Cockpit Usability
Yasuhiro Sugita, Masashi Mayumi, Mikihiro Noma, Ryoh Kikuchi, Yoshinori Ohhitsu,
Kenichiroh Tsuchida,Yasuyuki Togashi, Hiroshi Hasegawa
Sharp Display Technology Corporation, Mie, Japan
Abstract
We developed a Dual View Display for Center Information Displays (CID), increasing resolution from 74 ppi to 170 ppi for high-definition images visible from both driver and passenger sides. A dual-peak backlight greatly reduced crosstalk. We also integrated a touch system with User ID, enabling accurate and personalized operation from either position. This innovation is expected to improve cockpit usability.
Author Keywords
Dual View Display, CID, high resolution, dual-peak backlight, touch panel, ToF, User ID, automotive.
1. Objective and Background
Sharp marketed Dual View Displays for automotive applications from 2005 to 2019, with screen sizes ranging from 7.0 inches to 10.3 inches, and these displays were successfully implemented in production vehicles. In recent years, the growing popularity of electric vehicles (EVs) and the increasing digitalization of cockpits have led to a rise in the number of required displays, resulting in limited installation space. Consequently, the demand for Dual View Display technology -which enables two separate images to be displayed within the space of a single display -has increased.
The Dual View Display developed by our company for CID (Center Information Display) applications significantly increased resolution from the previous 74 ppi to 170 ppi, allowing two distinct high-definition images to be viewed from two directions (driver and passenger). Furthermore, by adopting a two-peak backlight instead of a conventional single-peak backlight, we achieved a significant reduction in crosstalk. Since CID applications require touch functionality for both driver and passenger screens, we developed a touch panel system equipped with operator identification capability, named “Dual View Touch.” This paper reports on the development and performance of this system.
2. Panel Structure of Dual View Display
The basic principle of the Dual View Display is simple. By forming a barrier layer with vertical black slits at twice the horizontal pixel pitch on the surface of the LCD panel, image separation using parallax becomes possible. With this structure, the driver can view only image A, while the passenger can view only image B. This means that two different images can be displayed simultaneously on a single screen, significantly enhancing the efficiency of information presentation within limited display space.
Furthermore, the developed prototype incorporates an in-cell touch panel display to enable touch interaction and adopts a two-peak backlight specifically designed for Dual View Display to improve visibility. (Figure. 1).
Figure 1. Structure and Principle of Dual View Display
3.1 Panel High-Resolution Enhancement
The first Dual View Display panel commercialized by Sharp in 2005 was a 7.0-inch model with a base resolution of 148 ppi. When a barrier composed of vertical slits was added, the effective resolution for Dual View Display was reduced by half to 74 ppi. As a result, the displayed images were of low resolution, and the vertical barrier lines became visible upon close observation, leading to degraded display quality.
In the newly developed panel, an 11.8-inch base panel with a resolution of 340 ppi was adopted, achieving an effective resolution of 170 ppi for Dual View Display. This improvement eliminates the visibility of vertical barriers and enables the display of highly detailed and visually appealing

3.2 Crosstalk Reduction
The most critical issue in Dual View Display systems is crosstalk, where images from the left and right overlap. When crosstalk occurs, the image intended to be black appears faintly transparent with content from the opposite view, resulting in a significant degradation of display quality.
In this study, we focused on optimizing the optical design to reduce crosstalk in Dual View Displays. Figure 2 shows a comparison of transmittance when both screens (A and B) are ON and when screen B is ON while screen A is OFF. As shown in Figure 2, the transmittance characteristics of Dual View Display are designed for automotive use, where the viewing angles are fixed at approximately ±40° based on seat positions. Therefore, both
screens are designed to have peak transmittance near ±40° when active. However, when screen A is black, the transmittance of screen B extends beyond the vertical direction (0°) into the A viewing area, which is the main cause of crosstalk.
Figure 2. Angular dependence of transmittance
in the Dual View Display
As a first step in addressing this issue, we examined the origin of transmittance leakage. Figure 3 shows a microscopic photograph of a single pixel (B+G+R) located directly beneath one barrier in an 11.8-inch panel, illuminated while all other pixels were off. Light emitted from the pixel beneath the barrier, which should normally be invisible, was observed from the front due to diffraction. This diffraction allows light to spread into angles that should be blocked by the black barrier, causing the B image to mix into the A image and appear as crosstalk to the A-side observer.
Figure 3. Photograph of a Single Pixel Illuminated
Beneath the Barrier
Through this study, it was clarified that the diffracted light generated by the black barrier constitutes the primary source of crosstalk. In addition, since the number of black barriers generally increases with higher display resolution, their impact can be expected to become more significant. (Figure 4) Furthermore, since it was determined that the diffraction effects induced by the black barrier cannot be completely eliminated in principle, we proceeded to investigate an alternative approach in which the angular distribution of the backlight, which is identified as the fundamental cause of the diffraction, is modified and optimized to suppress the generation of diffracted light itself.
Figure 4. Mechanism of Crosstalk Generation Caused by Diffracted Light
We utilized the fixed viewing angle characteristic of automotive applications and adopted a two-peak backlight structure, as shown in Figure 5. This design minimizes light emission toward the front and concentrates light toward ±40°, reducing crosstalk significantly. Figure 5 compares the angular luminance characteristics of a conventional single-peak backlight and the newly developed two-peak backlight for Dual View Display.
The two-peak backlight is designed to achieve maximum luminance at ±40°, corresponding to the driver and passenger viewing positions. Display panels are designed for automotive applications, where the viewing positions of the driver and passenger are fixed by seat location, and the observation angle is approximately ±40°. Therefore, both screens (A and B) are designed to have peak transmittance around ±40° when active.
Figure 5. Angular Luminance Characteristics of Conventional (1 peak) Backlight and 2 Peak Backlight
Figure 6 shows the angular dependence of crosstalk when the two-peak backlight is employed. Using the 2-peak backlight, the minimum crosstalk (①) is 0.3.At this condition, the Head Freedom, defined as the viewing region where crosstalk (XT) remains below 5 (②), exceeded 60°, while the Mixed Zone, defined as the region where XT exceeds 10 (③), was 20°. These results indicate that,
even in dark environments such as the interior of a vehicle at night, clear images without crosstalk can be observed, and the limited overlap between left and right images allows smooth image switching when the observer moves laterally.

4 Touch Panel and Operator Identification
The Dual View Display used in CID (Center Information Display) system is designed so that the driver and passenger can view different images simultaneously. Accordingly, individual touch operations corresponding to each displayed content are required. However, while the Dual View Display can separate video signals for left and right views, it traditionally employs a single touch panel, making it impossible to distinguish whether the input originated from the driver or the passenger side.
To address this limitation, we developed the “Dual View Touch System,” which incorporates a touch panel equipped with an
operator identification function, enabling input separation based on the user’s position (Figure. 7).
Two methods were developed for operator identification. In this section, we describe the differences between these two approaches.
Figure 7. Dual View Touch System
4.1 IR ToF (Time-of-Flight) Operator Identification System
This section describes the operator identification method using multi-zone IR (Infrared) ToF (Time of Flight) sensing. The system was developed in collaboration with STMicroelectronics, combining a ToF sensor (VL53L7CX) with the Dual View Display.
The sensor can measure distances within a diagonal field of view (DFOV) of 90° using a single unit. Furthermore, its multi-zone detection capability allows the measurement area to be divided into up to 64 blocks for distance detection.The sensing system uses infrared (IR) light at 940 nm and measures distance through differential processing. With this method, highly accurate distance measurement up to 65 cm is possible even under strong ambient light conditions in automotive environments.
Figure 8. structure of the IR ToF operator identification system
In this study, we propose a method for operator identification on an in-vehicle Dual View Display (CID) using two Time-of-Flight (ToF) sensors positioned around the periphery of the display. This configuration enables pre-detection of the direction from which the operator's fingers or arms approach, allowing the identification of the operator. Specifically, the operator is determined by combining (i) the detection area of the sensor blocks and (ii) the distance information. Since the seating positions in the vehicle cabin are fixed, the directions from which operators interact with the CID are limited, enabling rapid identification through a simple processing approach (Figure 9).
Figure 9. Diagram showing two IR ToF sensors measuring the distance to an object( finger)
This method does not require complex image processing, such as camera-based facial recognition, and can achieve detection over a wide field of view (approximately HFOV: 120°). Therefore, it is highly compatible with Dual View displays and can realize operator identification with low power consumption and low latency, compared to conventional complex recognition systems.
The pre-detection functionality is defined as Proximity Detection. The proximity detection information is immediately transmitted to the HOST, which uses the received data to determine the target
screen and control processing priority, thereby achieving low-latency and efficient system response.
Additionally, the proximity detection information is transmitted to the touch panel IC (TDDI). During touch input, the TDDI associates the proximity detection information with the touch input, generating operator identification information (User ID). The TDDI provides this User ID along with the touch input data to the HOST, which then controls the screen operation corresponding to the identified operator, ensuring that only the intended screen is manipulated (Figure. 10).
However, this method has a limitation: when multiple users perform touch operations simultaneously, the detection accuracy of the IR sensors decreases, making concurrent operation from both sides impractical. To address this issue, the development of a more accurate operator identification system was considered.
Figure 10. IR ToF System Flowchart
4. Pulse-Sheet Operator Identification System
We developed an operator identification system using a pulse generation method, based on a display module with an in-cell touch panel and Himax TDDI: HX83195A. The pulse generator is installed separately from the display module, on the driver’s or passenger’s seat. A specific frequency pulse is applied to the seat, and the signal is transmitted through the human body to the touch panel, enabling identification of which seat the operator is occupying (Figure. 11).
Figure 11. structure of the Pulse Seat operator identification system
The operator identification information (User ID) is determined by the TDDI and provided to the host system along with the touch input data. Subsequent processing by the host is performed in the same manner as with the ToF sensor System (Figure. 12).
