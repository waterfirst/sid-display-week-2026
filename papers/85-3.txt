Lens-Based CGEI Algorithm with GPU Acceleration for Integral Imaging
Light-Field Displays System
Shao-Yu Hung and Hoang-Yan Lin
Graduate Institute of Photonics and Optoelectronics, National Taiwan University, Taipei 106, Taiwan
Abstract

3. Lens-Based EI Pickup Model

Automotive Augmented Reality (AR) displays demand lowlatency elemental image generation for safety-critical cues.
However, conventional pixel-wise Computer Generated
Elemental Image (CGEI) methods are impractical for dynamic
scenes due to high computational costs. We propose a dynamic
synthesis pipeline integrating skeletonization-based trajectory
extraction with a lens-based LUT framework. By utilizing a
Unified Mask Stack and optimized PTX CUDA kernels, our
method achieves single-pass multi-depth synthesis. Experiments
show a generation time of just 0.032 s while maintaining high
image quality (SSIM > 0.91), validating the system’s potential for
approaching real-time automotive AR.

1. Introduction
Automotive augmented reality (AR) systems require low-latency
rendering for safety-critical scenarios; however, the
computational bottleneck of pixel-wise backward ray tracing in
traditional Computer-Generated Elemental Image (CGEI)
methods limits the deployment of light-field displays. While
inspired by prior camera multi-view acquisition research such as
Zhu et al. [1], such approaches necessitate rendering a dense array
of viewpoints, resulting in high latency insufficient for dynamic
driving environments. To address this, we propose a lens-based
CGEI method that utilizes precomputed geometric Look-Up
Tables (LUTs)—handling scaling, flipping, and padding—to
eliminate the prohibitive overhead of pixel-wise ray tracing. By
employing a GPU-parallelized framework with a mask stack and
optimized PTX CUDA kernels, we achieve single-pass multidepth synthesis with a generation time of just 0.032 seconds,
validating the system's potential for real-time deployment.
In the display architecture proposed in this study, a freeform
mirror is utilized to project light-field images, where dashboard
information is imaged at a distance of 2.5 meters, and arrows and
safety warnings are imaged at 7 meters. However, as the core of
this research focuses on the elemental image (EI) generation
technology for the light-field display itself, the image generation
depths discussed subsequently refer to millimeter-scale distances
within the microlens array (MLA) optical path, rather than the
final imaging distances after projection by the mirror.

4. GPU-Parallelized EI Synthesis Pipeline

3.3.2 Optical inversion
Following resizing, the template undergoes a centrosymmetric
inversion (horizontal and vertical flip), effectively producing a
180° rotation. This step compensates for the inherent image
reversal introduced by the real image formation of the microlens
array, ensuring that the spatial orientation of each extracted patch
matches the physical optical acquisition model.

4.1 Pixel-Parallel Architecture
Unlike the CPU implementation which iterates through microlens
tiles sequentially, our GPU framework adopts a fine-grained
parallel strategy. We map the compute grid directly to the display
resolution (𝑁𝑥 × 𝑁𝑦 ), assigning a single CUDA thread to each
physical pixel of the elemental image (e.g., 2160 × 2160 threads).
Each thread independently identifies its corresponding microlens
tile index (𝑖, 𝑗) and executes the depth-merging logic. This design
completely eliminates the overhead of nested loops on the host
side and ensures that the GPU compute cores are fully saturated.

4.2 LUT-Driven Geometric Access
To eliminate runtime arithmetic overhead, we pre-calculate all
depth-dependent spatial parameters—including extension factors
𝐸(𝑧), and tile sampling offsets—on the CPU and upload them to
the GPU as Look-Up Tables (LUTs). This approach replaces
costly floating-point operations (multiplication, division, and
rounding) within the kernel with efficient global memory fetches.
By decoupling geometric derivation from pixel rendering, the
kernel workload is streamlined into a branchless lookup process,
maximizing instruction throughput.

deviations at high-frequency boundaries. However, high SSIM
scores confirm that global structural fidelity and essential depth
cues are accurately preserved.

7. Conclusion
We proposed a dynamic, lens-based CGEI synthesis pipeline
leveraging skeletonization-based trajectory extraction and a
Unified Mask Stack to eliminate pixel-wise ray tracing overhead.
The implemented GPU-parallelized system generates highresolution (2160 x 2160) multi-depth EIs with an average latency
of 0.032s, achieving a >19x speedup over conventional methods
while maintaining robust structural fidelity (SSIM > 0.91). These
results validate the framework's potential as a viable rendering
engine for achieving approaching real-time performance in lightfield automotive HUDs.
