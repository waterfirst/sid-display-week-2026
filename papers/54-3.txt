1. Introduction

In recent years, as displays have become increasingly important
in automobiles, there has been a strong demand for large and
functional displays, such as pillar-to-pillar displays and curved
displays.
Improving visibility is the most critical factor for safety, and it is
essential to achieve high visibility regardless of ambient
brightness, the angle of incident light, or the viewing angle of
the display. Especially as displays become larger, the viewing
angles become wider; therefore, improving visibility across a
wide-angle range has become important.
Ambient Contrast Ratio (ACR) [1] has been proposed as a
method for evaluating image quality in bright environments. It is
calculated using the following formula, based on the Specular
Component Include (SCI) reflectance measured when the
display is off.

This research reports on the development of a wide-viewingangle AG film, a technology to suppress specular reflection by
optimizing internal reflections within the LCD panel, and a
high-brightness local dimming backlight using high-efficiency
Mini-LEDs. These innovations have enabled the realization of a
display with high image visibility regardless of the viewing
environment.

2. Evaluation of Reflective Properties
Environmental factors causing image quality degradation are a
decrease in contrast ratio due to specular reflection (glare) and
lower uniformity in luminance distribution across the screen.
This depends on the display's specular reflection characteristics
and is determined by the difference between Specular
Component Include (SCI) reflectance and Specular Component
Exclude (SCE) reflectance.
However, since SCI and SCE are methods that incident diffuse
light onto the panel and measure it from the panel normal
direction, they cannot evaluate the angular dependence of the
measured object's reflectance characteristics.
Generally, anti-reflective (AR) coatings and anti-glare (AG)
treatments exhibit angle-dependent characteristics, and it is
therefore necessary to evaluate their performance for light
incident at large angles. In this study, the gloss values at 20°,
60°, and 85° were measured using a gloss meter (GM-268Plus,
Konica Minolta) for various display panels. The results are
shown in Table 1.

Light reflection on displays, a factor reducing ACR, is classified
into specular reflection and diffuse reflection components.
Display panels exhibit particularly high specular reflection
components. Consequently, techniques to reduce surface
reflection and internal reflection within the panel have been
proposed as methods to improve ACR.

Next, we evaluated the luminance distribution within the image
when specular reflection occurred. A diffuse light-emitting panel
was used, illuminating the display at a 45-degree angle and
observed from the specular reflection direction. The illuminance
was 7,500 lux. For the glare panel, as shown in Fig. 1, light
source reflections appear, and the contrast ratio in part of the
image is decreased. In contrast, for the anti-glare panel using an
AG film, the reflected image of the light source is diffused,
resulting in a further decrease in luminance uniformity within
the screen. The problem of reduced luminance uniformity in the
anti-glare panel is caused by the narrowing of the light diffusion
angle due to the angle-dependent characteristics of the AG film's
light diffusion properties. This indicates that optimization of the
AG film's light diffusion characteristics is necessary.

4. Development of Low-Reflection LCD

We fabricated an LCD with low internal reflection [2-3]. We
suppressed refractive index mismatches between ITO and
adjacent layers, such as the glass substrate and polarizer
adhesive. We also optimized the refractive index of the black
resist used for the color filter's BM and the intermediate layer.
Furthermore, we optimized the TFT deposition sequence,
materials, and layer thicknesses. This resulted in suppression of
optical interference between multiple transparent insulating
layers, improving the internal reflectance of the panel from the
conventional 1.12% to 0.28%.
Additionally, a high-brightness local dimming backlight
utilizing mini-LEDs was developed to enhance visibility [4].
Key features include achieving higher brightness and improved
luminance uniformity by controlling the directionality of light
emission within zones using micro-optics. Furthermore, light
leakage into adjacent zones was suppressed, enabling localized
light emission while maintaining high contrast. This resulted in
image display with minimal halos and excellent contrast.
Finally, a high brightness of 3,000 cd/m² was achieved. The
specifications of the prototyped LED are shown in Table 3.

5. Conclusion

In this study, we developed an LCD panel that suppresses
internal reflections and a novel AGAR film with extremely low
incidence angle dependency and no sparkle effect to improve
display visibility in bright environments and over a wide
viewing angle range. By combining these technologies with a
high-brightness local dimming backlight, we successfully
realized an ultra-low specular reflection LCD with excellent
visibility that prevents image reflection even under extremely
bright conditions of 7,500 lux. This technology is expected to be
applied to various applications, including next-generation
automotive displays.
