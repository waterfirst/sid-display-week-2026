Abstract
Recently smart surface technology has been actively studied for the
automotive applications with hidden display systems emerging as a
pivotal component in this field. This study proposes an innovative
hidden display architecture that leverages the unique
characteristics of micro-LEDs—particularly their compact lightemitting area—to achieve high efficiency and high resolution.
Through proof-of-concept validation, we demonstrate the
feasibility and performance advantages of this proposed structure,
offering promising implications for next-generation automotive
display systems.

Introduction

Smart surface technology has recently emerged as a transformative
approach to vehicle human-machine interfaces (HM Is), offering
seamless integration of digital displays into automotive surfaces
while maintaining aesthetic appeal and functional versatility.
Within this rapidly evolving domain, hidden display systems have
gained prominence as a pivotal component, enabling innovative
visualization solutions that enhance both user experience and
vehicle design [1, 2].

2.

Proposal of New Hidden Display Structure

To enhance the optical efficiency of hidden displays, we focused
on a key characteristic of micro-LEDs: their small light-emitting
area. We redesigned the overall stacked structure of the hidden
display to minimize absorption along the optical path of the microLED light source. To this end, we developed a method that
decouples the two primary functional components of the hidden
display film—the background color and the foreground pattern—
by integrating the background color directly into the display panel
and fabricating the foreground pattern on a separate film for
subsequent attachment, as illustrated in Figure 3.

Conventional hidden displays have been fabricated by attaching a

transmittance of these films, a certain degree of performance
degradation in the underlying display remains unavoidable due to
the inherent structural limitations [1].

Proof of Concept

Preparation of Micro-LED panel: We selected a 10.4-inch
micro-LED panel with a resolution of 200 ppi to validate the high efficiency hidden display concept. Each pixel of this panel
incorporates two Φ10μm blue vertical micro-LED chips. Following
the completion of the LED electrode interconnection process, a
white reflective layer was deposited onto the panel. Using a
conventional photolithography process, this reflective layer was
patterned while considering the positions of the LED chips (see
Figure 4(b)). The white reflective layer serves two critical
functions: first, it enhances the visibility and accurate rendering of
colors, analogous to the function of white paper in printed media,
and second, it improves the light extraction efficiency of the microLED chips, thereby increasing overall luminous output.

During the PoC process, instead of newly developing a dedicated
color resin for achieving a specific hue, we opted to create a brownlike color by mixing existing red and green resins. By adjusting the
ratio of red to green, we were able to achieve a color closely
resembling brown (see Figure 5). In the future, it may be feasible
to develop and utilize more suitable color resins as needed,
depending on specific application requirements. The background
color was successfully formed on top of the white reflective layer
by coating the mixed color resin. This background color layer was
also patterned by photolithography process as well.

displayed image. It was confirmed that the foreground patterns of
transparent pattern film did not much interfere when the image was
activated by the bright micro-LEDs, resulting in clear and bright
image display as shown in Figure 7. The relative efficiency of final
PoC panel showed 96% of that of reference panel at the same power

Discussion and Conclusion

Through the PoC process and results of this study, it was
confirmed that the proposed micro-LED display structure reduces
light absorption by the hidden display film layer, leading to higher
efficiency while maintaining the aesthetic appeal required for
hidden displays when the power is off. The findings of this
research are significant because they improve power efficiency —
a known weakness of existing hidden displays—and suggest the
potential for a display structure that is better suited for highresolution image representation.
