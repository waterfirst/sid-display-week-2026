Abstract 
With AI enabling the unthought of a decade ago, such 
as humanoid robotics or large language models, it is 
easy to overlook that AI can also replace or augment 
the most traditional computation tasks such as video 
processing. Video compression innovation seeks to 
improve the ratio of video quality to bandwidth: three 
different approaches to using AI target to improve this 
efficiency. Whilst innovation in video scaling can

compression and scaling for displays can enable 
thinking differently about the video preparation stage 
for AI pipelines to provide performance improvement 
(speed up) and more accurate classification.

1. Introduction  
An insatiable audience and creator expectation for higher quality 
visual experiences helps drive display innovation through higher 
resolution, frame rates, dynamic range and more. To take 
advantage of this increasing quality of display it is ever more 
important to ensure the highest quality is maintained from the 
video origin (e.g. from RAW form) through to delivery, the 
techniques to support this are referred to as video processing.   
This paper will delve into the fascinating field of the use of AI 
to replace or augment traditional video processing which is a 
fundamental part of the computation within display systems. 
The authors follow the sentiment of [1] in the title quote “the 
most successful AI right now is what I call boring AI”, that 
whilst AI-based video processing is not the sexiest of AI 
applications it is practical and has potential for high scale usage.  
Whilst there are many video processing functions, video 
compression and video scaling are the focus of this paper as two 
of the most computationally intensive operations. 
As a domain of focus for the authors, the application of the 
results to applications in Professional Display Systems will be 
specifically considered in the paper.    
The paper is structured as follows: Section 2 surveys prior work; 
Target compute types for video processing are discussed in 
Section 3; Section 4 presents results showing that a super 
resolution implementation first intended for GPU-accelerator 
cards can run efficiently on low power AI accelerators such as 
NPUs or integrated GPUs; The application of video processing 
innovation to AI vision pipelines is presented in Section 5;  In 
Section 6 the works are summarized; Thoughts on the greater 
impact of the research are presented in Section 7.   
2. Survey of Prior Work 
The use of AI in video processing tasks is not a new one. [2] 
presents a comprehensive literature survey of the role of AI in 
the full content delivery pipeline stating applications from video 
encoding to decoding to the delivery network. From an edge the 
potential for energy efficiency during transmission (due to lower 
bit rates) is an interesting observation motivating the value of 
the AI innovations across different video processing tasks.  
2.1. Super Resolution 
It is interesting to consider the role of super resolution (SR) 
across various applications. SR is used with video games for a 
game engine to render the game play at lower resolution and 
frame rate with inbuilt GPU technology such as XeSS2 [3] to 
upscale resolution and frame rate for a high-resolution gaming 
experience at a lower GPU performance. In [4], Lei and Jiang 
present the application in social media with interesting insights 
on use to improve user generated content as cloud-based 
processing and on device SR upscaling at edge of network. This 
work leveraged open-source SR code from [5]. This [6] API

poor networks and bandwidth optimization to improve content

performance scaling up to 1440p using an NPU. Latest 8K TV 
specifications [7] show support for SR within the display 
chipset, which is cited as important to take advantage of the 
higher display resolution whilst available content is still either 
FHD or 4K [8]. [9] shows an example of how SR can augment a 
bi-cubic filter with focus of the AI to add back residuals such as 
edge sharpness, it also shows broader application to processing 
including SDR to HDR conversion.    The common value 
propositions across these use cases are to improve quality 
(adding details that are not present in upscaling such as by bi-
cubic) and/or optimizing network bandwidth or compute need. 
There has so far been an expectation, such as enabling SR in 
Microsoft Edge [10] requiring adding an Nvidia or AMD GPU 
card, of the need for acceleration. The results in [6] for up to 
1440p and those shown in Section 4 for scaling up to 4K 
resolution show this need not be the case.     
Hallucinations of AI are a common concern when discussing its 
use for video scaling or CODECs. In [11] an interesting 
approach to SR with AI is taken to use AI to pre-train filters and 
includes guard circuitry to help protect against anomalies.  
2.2. AI Video CODECs  
A fundamental task of video compression is to get the highest 
quality video signal to the destination at the lowest possible 
bandwidth, referred to as compression efficiency. AI approaches 
to video compression, such as [12-15], aim to improve upon this

For video CODECs it is interesting to consider 3 roles for AI. 
First, pre or post-processing video with AI-based approaches for 
existing CODECs. Second, AI-based approaches to traditional 
CODEC tools. Thirdly, where the whole video CODEC is 
rethought to use AI throughout the processing.  
As example of the first approach, [14] demonstrated the use of 
SEI messages with VCC/H.266 to provide a 5% bit-rate 
reduction 
using 
AI-based 
post-processing 
with 
model 
information sent in the SEI message. In [15], the concept of AI-
based pre-processing to implicitly influence CODEC saliency 
map is another good example of this first type. [16] is a similar 
example from real-world deployment at the Paris Olympics 
whereby an AI approach was taken to preset the CODEC to the 
AI-perceived type of content and best-known settings. 
As brief definition, saliency maps are how a CODEC determines 
where to allocate the most bits in compression to be sympathetic 
to how the human visual system will view the decoded video.  
On the second type, examples include proposals to H.267 [17] or 
the MPAI-Enhanced Video Coding (EVC) [18] standardization 
efforts. [15] compares the implicit approach to explicitly guiding 
the saliency map with an AI-approach as a documented example 
of how CODECs may be modified with AI techniques.  
Examples of the third approach include Deep Render, recently 
acquired by Interdigital, where testing [12] shows capability to 
run 1080p video decoding with their AI approach on an Apple 
Mac Mini M4 38TOP NPU at 69fps with quality higher than 
AV1 or H.265 in tested bit streams (slightly lower quality than 
VVC). MPAI-EEV E2E is an example standardization AI-based 
CODEC project [13]. An interesting observation is that such an 
AI-based CODEC, like will be shown with the SR testing in 
Section 4, can potentially be run within modern NPUs such as 
the upcoming Intel Core Ultra 3[19], leaving the GPU free for 
graphics or heavy workload AI allowing CODEC innovation to 
be an NPU software change versus need for silicon changes.  
[20] shows reduced complexity of AI CODEC for 1080p30 
coding, claiming 20x speedup for DCVC-RT versus DCVC-
DC/DCVC-FM running on NVIDIA RTX 2080 Ti.

3. Compute Types for Edge Display Systems 
In display systems, video processing is a fundamental 
requirement in the overall dataflow. [21] presented that there are 
four types of computer hardware, with (example processor 
types) which are commonly used to support processing 
associated with display applications. These being scalar (CPU), 
vector (GPU), matrix (NPU) and spatial (FPGA or ASIC). It is 
notable that the vector and spatial types are what historically 
computed video processing tasks such as video CODECs, 
scaling and other pre/post-processing. It will be seen later in this 
paper that matrix types such as neural processing units (NPUs) 
are destined to become a third common target.  
Modern processors today such as Intel Core Ultra 3[19] or 
Apple M5 [22] include these different compute engine types 
within a single packaged processor.

In modern on-device AI workloads, understanding how model 
performance scales with input resolution and numerical 
precision are crucial for selecting the right hardware and 
optimization strategy. From the standpoint of system design, the 
behaviour of a Super Resolution model across devices illustrates 
a more general principle: the same AI algorithm can occupy 
very different points in the trade-off space of quality, speed, and 
hardware constraints. A configuration that comfortably runs on a 
GPU may, when quantized and paired with a lower-resolution 
input, still be viable on an embedded NPU or CPU, yet remain 
good enough for different scenarios. This flexibility suggests 
that video compression and scaling should not be treated as 
fixed, but as tuneable components that can be adapted to the 
capabilities of each deployment target while still improving both 
perceived visual quality and the effectiveness of downstream AI 
tasks. The results in Figure 1 demonstrate this solutions space.  
Notably at the highest performance, real-time (>60fps) 1080p to 
4K scaling is achievable on the integrated GPU at int8 precision. 
For the NPU (which has 13 TOPS [23] peak performance) this 
achieves 20fps performance so it can be projected with 50 TOPS 
on upcoming Intel processors [19] real-time could be possible.

5. Application to AI Vision Pipelines 
Figure 2 shows a typical AI Vision Pipeline, concurrent camera 
streams are decoded, this data needs pre-processing (often 
downscaling) before object detection, after which further 
cropping and scaling is needed to prepare objects to be classified 
as different detections, finally the results are overlayed on the 
original video content before display. To date the majority focus 
for performance and accuracy improvement has been on model 
innovation in the non-shaded boxes. In this section options for 
innovation in the shaded boxes, by applying the AI-based video 
processing are considered.

 One effective way to save bandwidth in such pipelines is to 
transmit a downscaled video stream and then rely on super-
resolution at the receiver. To ensure that detector performance is 
maintained or even improved on these reconstructed videos, AI-
based super-resolution models can be used. Building on this 
pipeline view, we next ask how changes in the shaded stages, 
compression and scaling before detection, affect the accuracy of 
the non-shaded detection model. Low-bitrate compression 
typically introduces artifacts that harm downstream object 
detection, so we quantified this effect by measuring mean 
Average Precision (mAP) with YOLOv11n, a lightweight 
detector suitable for real-time multi-stream scenarios such as 
drone body cams. We considered three transmission strategies 
that correspond to different choices in the “preparation” part of 
the pipeline: (i) a reference stream at full resolution (1080p) and 
1000 kbps (H.265), (ii) a more aggressively compressed 1080p 
stream at 700 kbps, and (iii) a 540p stream at 700 kbps that is 
then upscaled back to 1080p using our Super Resolution 
pipeline before entering the detector. As summarized in Table 4, 
simply reducing the bitrate from 1000 to 700 kbps at full 
resolution leads to a measurable drop in mAP of about one 
percentage point, consistent with the expected impact of 
compression artifacts. By contrast, the 540p @ 700 kbps + AI 
Super Resolution configuration recovers essentially the same 
mAP as the 1000 kbps baseline (and slightly exceeds it on one 
clip), despite using 30% less bandwidth. These preliminary 
results suggest that innovation in the shaded boxes of the 
pipeline, here, AI-based scaling and reconstruction, can partially 
“undo” the damage of low-bitrate compression and optimize 
bandwidth usage without compromising detection performance.

6. Summary  
This document explores how artificial intelligence (AI) 
enhances traditional video processing tasks such as compression 
and scaling, particularly for edge display systems including 
professional displays. It highlights the practical benefits of AI-
driven video processing in improving video quality, reducing 
bandwidth, and optimizing AI vision pipelines on various 
hardware types. Here are some summary findings.  
• AI augments video processing tasks: AI can replace or enhance 
video compression and scaling to improve video quality and 
bandwidth efficiency, supporting applications like digital 
signage and unified communications.  
• Super resolution applications: AI-based super resolution (SR) 
improves image detail beyond traditional upscaling, supports 
bandwidth optimization, and can run efficiently on edge devices 
without requiring high-end GPU accelerators.  
• AI-based video codecs: Three AI codec approaches exist: AI 
post-processing with traditional codecs, AI-enhanced traditional 
codec tools, and fully AI-based codecs running on neural 
processing units (NPUs), offering improved compression

efficiency and quality.  
• Edge compute types for video processing: Video processing 
leverages scalar (CPU), vector (GPU) and matrix (NPU) AI 
acceleration in modern processors to enable flexible AI video 
processing deployments at different optimization points.  
• Performance on standard processors: Implementations of super 
resolution originally designed for GPUs can achieve real-time 
1080p to 4K scaling on integrated GPUs and promising 
performance on NPUs, expanding edge processing capabilities 
without dedicated GPU accelerators.  
• Enhancing AI vision pipelines: AI video processing 
innovations in compression and scaling stages can reduce 
bandwidth and improve object detection accuracy in vision 
pipelines, partially mitigating low-bitrate compression artifacts. 
• Super Resolution in AI vision pipelines: Early results 
demonstrate that super resolution can add back lost detail in high 
compression (such as down to 700 kbps) to achieve accuracy of 
result of higher bit rate compression (example at 1000 kbps). 
As future work the authors plan to perform a deeper analysis of 
the benefits of super resolution on AI pipelines. Also to analyse 
in more detail the performance of AI enabled video CODECs to 
map out performance capability on current and future processors 
as well as understand the value of these for AI pipelines. 
7. Industry Impact Statement 
This paper motivates us to think differently about the role of AI 
in the most traditional video processing tasks. The research 
indicates that AI-driven video processing can improve 
perceptual video quality and resource efficiency, influencing 
future developments in large format IoT display system 
applications. The work also motivates that innovation in the 
video preparation stages of AI pipelines with AI-based video 
processing shows promise to optimise quality of result and/or 
video bandwidth requirements.
