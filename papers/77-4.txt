Dual-SLM Tiled Holographic HUD with Dammann Grating for Wide Eye-box
Jinsu Lee, Han-Ju Yeom, and KeeHoon Hong
* Digital Holography Research Section, Electronics and Telecommunications Research Institute,
218 Gajeong-ro, Yuseong-gu, Daejeon, Republic of Korea

Abstract
We propose a binocular holographic automotive head-up display
(HUD) architecture designed to overcome the limited spacebandwidth product of spatial light modulators (SLMs). The
proposed system utilizes dual-SLM tiling to double the horizontal
spatial bandwidth and a custom-optimized 5×5 Dammann grating
to achieve a 25-fold expansion of the eye-box area. The feasibility
of the optical design was verified through comprehensive
simulations. Geometric optical analysis using Zemax OpticStudio
confirmed that the projection optics achieve near-diffractionlimited performance with minimal distortion. Furthermore, waveoptical simulations based on the angular spectrum method
demonstrated that the Dammann grating generates highly uniform
pupil replicas with a root-mean-square (RMS) uniformity error of
1.5%, even under realistic 8-bit phase quantization constraints.
This study presents a scalable and robust optical design framework
for next-generation wide-field-of-view augmented reality HUDs.

2. Optical Configuration
The proposed system employs a hybrid optical architecture
combining dual-SLM tiling, bandwidth scaling via 4-f optics, and
diffractive pupil replication. The schematic configuration is
designed to maximize the angular bandwidth while maintaining a
compact volume suitable for automotive integration.

2.1.

Head-up displays (HUDs) have become essential in modern
vehicles, enhancing situational awareness and reducing cognitive
load by projecting critical information directly into the driver’s
forward field of view [1]. Conventional HUDs based on TFT-LCD
or DLP projection typically rely on bulky freeform combiners and
fixed-focus optics. Consequently, their field of view (FOV), eyebox size, and depth reproduction capabilities are fundamentally
constrained by the geometric limitations of refractive optics. As
demand grows for augmented reality (AR) in advanced driverassistance systems (ADAS), next-generation HUDs require
significantly wider eye-boxes and flexible 3D visualization
capabilities that traditional architectures cannot support.
Holographic displays have emerged as a promising alternative,
offering direct wavefront control to reconstruct depth-varying
virtual content within compact optical architectures [2]. Computergenerated holography (CGH) using phase-only spatial light
modulators (SLMs) enables dynamic focusing and optical
distortion compensation, making it a compelling approach for
automotive applications. However, a critical challenge remains: the
limited space-bandwidth product (SBP) of current SLMs results in
inherently narrow eye-boxes, severely restricting the driver’s head
movement range. To address this, various pupil-replication
methods have been proposed, primarily for near-eye displays. Choi
et al. demonstrated a two-dimensional eye-box replication
technique using holographic optical elements (HOEs) and angularspectrum wrapping. Similarly, computational Maxwellian displays
have employed multiplexed off-axis plane-wave encoding to form
pupil arrays while maintaining compactness. Recent studies have
also explored multi-angle illumination with pupil-aware
optimization to expand the eye-box without filters, improving
motion tolerance. Despite these advances, most prior work targets
wearable near-eye displays rather than automotive HUDs.
Furthermore, existing approaches typically rely on a single SLM,

Dual-SLM Optical Tiling

To overcome the limited active area of a single SLM, two phaseonly SLMs are arranged horizontally and optically combined using
a polarizing cube beam splitter and tilted mirror as shown in Fig. 1.
This configuration allows the left and right SLMs to function as a
single continuous aperture with doubled horizontal width.
Specifically, the left SLM encodes the hologram for the left eye,
and the right SLM for the right eye, ensuring physical separation of
the binocular channels at the source. This tiling method effectively
doubles the space-bandwidth product (SBP) without introducing
polarization-dependent imbalances.

Chromatic Aberration and Eye-Relief Shift

A significant result of the grating's dispersion is the wavelengthdependent shift in the eye-relief distance (eye-box location). As
shown in Fig. 2, the optimal eye-relief is located at 680 nm for the
blue wavelength and 730 mm for the red wavelength as shown in
Fig. 2. Due to the chromatic dispersion of the Dammann grating,
the reconstructed image exhibits a distinct color shift as the
observation position moves from 680 mm to 720 mm, transitioning
from a bluish to a reddish tint. This confirms that the focal planes
for the RGB channels are spatially separated along the optical axis.
Hence, to evaluate the static optical performance, we established a
reference observation plane at the intermediate distance of 705 mm.

3. Dammann Grating Design
The core component enabling the 25-fold eye-box expansion is the
Dammann grating, a binary or multi-level phase grating designed
to distribute incident optical power uniformly into a specific array
of diffraction orders. In this work, we targeted a 5x5 array
corresponding to diffraction orders (𝑚, 𝑛) where 𝑚, 𝑛 in
{−2, −1, 0, +1, +2}.

3.1.

Design Algorithm

The phase profile was optimized using an Iterative Fourier
Transform Algorithm (IFTA) adapted for equal-power generation.
To ensure computational efficiency and fabrication simplicity, we
adopted a separable design approach. Independent 1D phase
profiles, 𝜙𝑥 (𝑥) and 𝜙𝑦 (𝑦) , were first optimized to generate 5
uniform orders in each direction. The final 2D phase map Φ(x, y)
was then constructed by superimposing the two orthogonal
profiles:

The optimization constraints were set to minimize the uniformity
error among the target orders while maximizing diffraction
efficiency. To match the aspect ratio of the automotive eye-box
requirements, the spatial frequencies were set to 𝑁𝑥 = 49 𝑙𝑖𝑛𝑒𝑠/
𝑚𝑚 and 𝑁𝑦 = 14 𝑙𝑖𝑛𝑒𝑠/𝑚𝑚, corresponding to grating periods of
approximately 20.4𝜇𝑚 and 71.4𝜇𝑚, respectively.

3.2.

Numerical Verification

The performance of the designed grating was rigorously verified
using wave-optical simulations based on the angular spectrum
method (ASM). Fig. 3 shows the resulting far-field intensity
distribution and the cross-section plot at 𝑦 = 0. The simulation
confirmed that the 25 diffracted spots are generated with high
contrast and uniform intensity. The quantitative analysis yielded an
RMS uniformity error of 15.52% and a Peak-to-Valley (PV)
deviation of 40.2% at the design wavelength ( 𝜆 = 650𝑛𝑚 ).
Furthermore, multi-wavelength simulations (RGB) demonstrated
that while the diffraction angles scale with wavelength, the 5x5
array structure remains intact, supporting full-color operation
within the expanded eye-box. It is worth noting that the observed
intensity roll-off is primarily attributed to the sinc-envelope effect
caused by the discrete pixel structure of the SLM relative to the
grating period. Since the high-order diffraction efficiency naturally
decays due to the finite pixel fill factor, the central order exhibits
higher intensity than the peripheral orders. However, unlike
random speckle noise, this non-uniformity is spatially continuous
and deterministic. Therefore, it can be effectively compensated
numerically by applying an inverse-intensity mask during the
hologram generation process. By pre-calibrating the target intensity
of the CGH, the system can deliver a perceptually uniform
luminance across the entire expanded eye-box in practical
implementation.

3.3.

Experimental Verification

To experimentally validate the proposed Dammann grating design,
we fabricated a master stamp using photolithography and evaluated
its optical diffraction performance. The fabrication aimed to realize
the optimized phase profile derived in Section 3 on a physical
substrate. A 95𝑚𝑚 𝑥 95𝑚𝑚 aperture area was patterned on a
photoresist layer spin-coated on a glass substrate. The phase depth
was controlled by the exposure time and development process,
targeting a 𝜙 phase shift at the design wavelength (𝜆 = 650𝑛𝑚).
While nano-imprint lithography was considered for mass
production, initial trials indicated that the replication process
introduced significant handling errors and etch-depth mismatches
(shrinkage), which distorted the zero-order intensity. Therefore, to
accurately assess the validity of the optical design itself,
independent of replication artifacts, we performed the optical
evaluation directly on the PR master stamp. The optical
reconstruction was analyzed using a collimated laser source (𝜆 =
650𝑛𝑚). Fig. 4 shows the diffraction pattern captured from the
fabricated PR master. The measured relative intensities of the 5x5
diffraction orders are summarized in Table 2. The measurement
shows a mean intensity of 2.45 with a standard deviation of 0.74.
The zero-order intensity was measured at 4.4, which is higher than
the average. However, the overall energy distribution demonstrates
a clear 5x5 grid structure with sufficient uniformity for holographic
imaging.

4. Hologram Generation Stage
To validate the proposed optical architecture and ensure highquality 3D image reconstruction within the expanded eye-box, we
developed a comprehensive wave-optical simulation framework.
This framework integrates a Fourier-domain hologram synthesis
with a rigorous ABCD ray-matrix analysis to verify the wavefront
curvature and ensure precise retinal focusing.

4.1.

Fourier Hologram Synthesis and
Virtual Source Control

The holographic signal generation is based on the Fresnel
diffraction model. The spatial light modulator (SLM) is illuminated
by a complex field generated from the target image. To compensate
for the strong refractive power of the subsequent 4-lens relay
system and to form a virtual image at a comfortable viewing
distance, a specific Virtual Source Distance is applied at the SLM
plane. The complex field on the SLM, 𝑈𝑠𝑙𝑚 , is synthesized by
transforming the target image 𝐼𝑡𝑎𝑟𝑔𝑒𝑡 and modulating it with a

the final wavefront arriving at the eye is divergent (virtual image)
and falls within the accommodation range of the driver. The optical
propagation through the multi-lens system is numerically modeled
using the angular spectrum method and Fresnel transfer functions.

4.2.

Optimization of Superimposed CGHs
with Nested Eye-box

The final stage of the pipeline addresses the signal integrity within
the "nested" eye-box structure created by the overlapping

diffraction orders. Since the Dammann grating generates a 5x5
array of replicated viewing zones, the driver's pupil often captures
a superposition of adjacent diffraction orders rather than a single
isolated order. To maximize image quality under these conditions,
our algorithm performs a wavelength-specific signal optimization
at the detected pupil coordinates. The total optical field entering the
eye is modeled as the sum of the shifted wavefields from the
contributing diffraction orders. The simulation analyzes this
superimposed signal intensity for each RGB wavelength
independently. By evaluating the constructive and destructive
interference patterns within the pupil, the system iteratively adjusts
the initial hologram amplitude to ensure that the reconstructed
image intensity matches the target distribution. This process
effectively minimizes the non-uniformity caused by the discrete
diffraction envelop and ensures consistent color balance and
brightness even when the eye traverses the boundaries between
replicated view zones.

5. Conclusion
In this study, we proposed a dual-SLM holographic HUD
architecture integrated with a 5x5 Dammann grating, successfully
achieving a 25-fold expansion of the eye-box while overcoming the
space-bandwidth product limitations. Both geometric and waveoptical simulations verified the system's optical feasibility,
demonstrating near-diffraction-limited performance and high
uniformity of the replicated pupils. Although inherent chromatic
aberrations and distortions were observed, they were effectively
mitigated through our proposed pupil-adaptive CGH calibration
scheme, ensuring clear and color-balanced 3D visualization.
Consequently, this scalable architecture offers a practical and
robust solution for next-generation wide-field-of-view automotive
AR HUDs.
