Laminated and Heterogeneous Integration for Opto-electronics via Nanoprinting
Zhixuan Zhao*, Tianshuo Zhao*, **
* Department of Electrical and Electronic Engineering, The University of Hong Kong, Hong Kong SAR,
China
** Materials Innovation Institute for Life Sciences and Energy (MILES), HKU-SIRI, Shenzhen, P.R. China
Abstract
We established a nano-resolution printing method to deposit
nanocrystal (NC) inks and modify material properties via in-situ
room-temperature ligand-exchange. This enables heterogeneous
integration of multi-layer opto-electronic devices on low-thermalresistance substrates. We printed metal (Au, Ag) and
semiconductors (ZnO, PbS, CdSe, and InSb) NC structures,
achieving 70-nm linewidths with competitive (opto)electronic
characteristics for micro-nano devices.

Author Keywords
nano-printing; heterogeneous integration; room temperature;
ligand-exchange; low-thermal-resistance substrate.

1. Introduction
The exponential proliferation of the Internet of Things (IoT),
wearable technology, autonomous systems, and augmented and
virtual reality (AR/VR) has rendered ubiquitous sensing and
computing a reality, thereby precipitating a demand for highperformance,
application-specific
optoelectronics.1,2
The
realization of such customizable devices necessitates engineered
multi-material
constituents,
adaptable
manufacturing
methodologies, and flexible heterogeneous integration strategies.
While additive manufacturing facilitates the fabrication of bespoke,
multiscale structures via functional inks, current techniques remain
constrained. For instance, although stereolithography attains submicrometer resolution, its material palette is primarily restricted to
metal oxides and polymers, limiting its utility largely to mechanical
and optical components.3,4 Consequently, fully printed, pixelated
microelectronics—which require precise semiconductor patterning
and multilayer stacking—remain at a nascent stage of development.
Addressing these challenges necessitates a adjustable ink system
integrated with versatile printing protocols. Colloidal nanocrystals
(NCs) are promising candidates, having demonstrated efficacy in
flexible circuits and photodetectors. Their crystalline core-shell
structure offers tailorable properties, particularly the size-tunable
optoelectronics of NCs.5,6 Crucially, ligand-exchange (L-E)
substitutes insulating ligands with compact alternatives to enable
charge transport.7 Moreover, selective ligand chemistry allows for
the engineering of carrier mobility, doping, and energy levels. This
facilitates the layer-by-layer assembly of complex devices while
mitigating micro-defects associated with ligand loss, thereby
supporting robust multi-material integration.
Electrohydrodynamic printing (EHDP) enables nanoscale
resolution with diverse ink materials, overcoming nozzle diameter
limitations.8 However, conventional high-temperature sintering is
often deleterious to heat-sensitive semiconductive NCs and flexible
substrates.9,10 While low-temperature chemical treatments have
been used to deposit NCs as sensitizers on graphene11 for infrared
detection, achieving scalable all-printed NC optoelectronics
requires deeper understanding of NC-surface ligand interactions
and multi-material integration.
We present a ligand-exchange-assisted EHDP) protocol enabling

sub-micron NC patterning with in-situ room-temperature
functionalization. This method facilitates the heterogeneous
integration of Ag, Au, ZnO, and PbS NCs, achieving linewidths of
70 nm and Ag conductivities of 1.99×107 S/m via post-exchange
densification. Beyond fabricating structural color gratings and
metasurfaces, we demonstrate multi-layer IR photodiodes featuring
10-µm pixels composed of n- and p-doped PbS NCs with
asymmetric contacts. Under 1500 nm illumination, these devices
exhibit enhanced rectification, reduced dark currents, and rapid
response times compared to standard benchmarks. This work
establishes a viable room-temperature pathway for processing
sensitive materials in advanced optoelectronics.

2. Mechanisms of L-E assisted nano-printing of
NC assemblies
Colloidal NCs are typically synthesized with long organic capping
ligands, which render them stable as dispersions in non-polar
solvents—a critical prerequisite for formulating printable inks
compatible with EHDP. As illustrated in Fig. 2a, a diverse library
of NC core materials can be accessed through this approach. This
library encompasses metals, semiconductors (including quantum
dots), and metal oxides, all synthesized with surface ligands such
as oleic acid (OA) or oleylamine (OLAM). These ligands confer
solubility and colloidal stability in non-polar solvent systems like
dodecane, thereby enabling the controlled deposition required for
high-resolution additive manufacturing.12 They are deposited by
EHDP similar with previous research,8 which utilizes pulsed
voltages to generate electric forces that overcome ink surface
tension and viscosity, enabling droplet ejection from the nozzle tip.
As illustrated in Fig. 1, by translating the stage of the custom-built
EHD printer along a defined path, NCs are patterned into linear
structures on the substrate (Fig. 2b). These printed lines serve as
fundamental building blocks that can be spatially organized and
combined through programmed stage movements to form
customized patterns and arbitrarily shaped thin films.

Applying a L-E condition of 1 mg/mL NH4SCN or TBAI for 60 s
induces significant volumetric shrinkage in printed structures.
Specifically, NH4SCN treatment reduced Ag NC linewidths from
651.4 ± 49 nm to 274.5 ± 31 nm, while TBAI exchange PbS NC
lines from 754.9 ±65 nm to 544.0 ±34 nm. This room-temperature
assembly yields dense, electronically coupled sub-micron solids
with optoelectronic properties comparable to spin-coated
benchmarks. The resulting Ag and PbS NC lines and films
demonstrate optoelectronic properties on par with their spin-coated
counterparts, the underlying mechanisms and optimization of
which will be detailed in subsequent sections.

We draw on the exceptional nanoscale patterning capability and
high electrical conductivity of printed silver nanocrystal (Ag NC)

(SCs). These gratings spatially decompose incident white light into

spacing from 600 nm to 1900 nm in 100 nm increments, enabling
the generation of high-purity structural colors, as illustrated in Fig.

broader palette of structural colors can be achieved, with the

green, and blue (RGB) primary color gratings (Fig. 5). For

a distinct white coloration. In comparison to recently reported

greater tunability of line spacing afforded by our method, all while

4. Optimization of Printed Pattern Conductivity
Upon chemical treatment of nano-printed NC structures, we
observe that grain growth induced by L-E is governed more
decisively by the initial ink concentration used during the EHDP
process. This concentration directly influences the dynamic
detachment of ligands from NC surfaces. As described by the
Allen-Cahn equation, the remaining surface-bound ligands
establish the driving force for subsequent grain growth.12
Integrating experimental data with computational modeling reveals
that a substantial fraction of organic ligands detach during EHDP
when higher-concentration NC inks are employed. This predeposition ligand loss leads to the formation of larger NC
aggregates upon printing, consequently reducing the driving force
for chemically driven sintering during the subsequent L-E step. By
systematically optimizing the initial ink concentration, we
successfully minimized ligand detachment during printing, thereby
preserving a higher effective NC concentration. This optimization
not only enhances printing efficiency but also markedly improves
the filling density and structural integrity of the resulting
nanocrystal architectures.
The electrical performance of these structures was evaluated by
measuring the DC conductivity of Ag NC films using the four-point
probe method. Peak conductivity, reaching 1.99 × 10⁷ S/m, was
achieved at an optimized ink concentration of 3.0 mg/mL.

5. Multi-material Laminated Printing for Optoelectronics Fabrication and Heterogeneous
Integration
Beyond Ag NCs as printing system, the ligand-exchange (L-E)assisted nanoprinting platform demonstrates broad material
versatility. Its applicability extends to a range of functional
colloidal inks, including semiconductive PbS NCs, InSb NCs,
CdSe NCs and dielectric ZnO NCs, showcased with the NH4SCN
L-E to realise various pattern designs at sub-micron resolution,

Leveraging our L-E-assisted nanoprinting platform, we fabricated
sub-10 µm PbS NC patterns and integrated photodiode (PD)
devices entirely under ambient conditions. In contrast to
single-doped PbS NC photoconductors, the photodiodes reported
here incorporate built-in P–N junctions. These junctions are
realized through the distinct doping characteristics imparted by
different ligand treatments: EDT induces p-type doping in PbS
NCs, while TBAI or PbI₂ yields n-type behavior.15,16 To construct
the multi-layer, multi-material device architecture, we first printed
bottom electrode pairs using Ag and Au NC inks—each processed
with NH₄SCN-assisted ligand exchange. Subsequently, a layer of
TBAI-treated n-type PbS NCs was printed over the Ag electrode,
followed by a layer of EDT-treated p-type PbS NCs printed to
establish direct contact with the Au electrode, as illustrated
schematically in Fig. 7.

Figure 7. Schematics of all-printed PbS NC IR photodiodes
(left), energy band diagram (right top), optical microscopy
images (right bottom).
To realize a fully printed nanocrystal photodiode, we incorporated
ZnO NC as the n-type electron transport layer. The device
architecture consists of multiple NCs layers laterally bridging a pair
of pre-printed Au and Ag electrodes, both of which have undergone
SCN⁻-based ligand exchange. Fabrication proceeds through
sequential deposition of PbS-EDT, PbS-TBAI, and ZnO-SCN
layers, each followed by its corresponding ligand treatment. The
ZnO layer is positioned atop the PbS stack, serving to electrically
isolate it from the underlying Ag electrode. The resulting
conduction path follows Au-SCN / PbS-EDT / PbS-TBAI /
ZnO-SCN / Ag-SCN, supporting both in-plane charge transport
within individual layers and vertical carrier movement across the
heterojunction interfaces, as schematically depicted in Fig. 8a.
Upon illumination with 1480 nm light, the photodiode exhibits an
open-circuit voltage of 0.22 V and achieves a remarkable external
quantum efficiency of 49%-56.1 % at a –4 V bias. The built-in
electric field established across the device (Fig. 8b) promotes
efficient charge separation, yielding a response time below
20 ms—a value that exceeds the temporal resolution limit of our

6. Conclusion
We report a general approach for the layer-by-layer printing and
chemical functionalization of NC structures. In contrast to
conventional inkjet printing followed by high-temperature
sintering, our method integrates nano-resolution EHDP with
room-temperature L-E. This synergistic combination substantially
broadens the palette of compatible NC inks, substrate materials,
and attainable device functionalities. Crucially, the entirely
room-temperature process preserves thermally sensitive materials
and facilitates the heterogeneous integration of disparate
components on diverse substrate platforms.
Using Ag NCs as a model system, we systematically investigated
the printing parameters and elucidated the mechanism underlying
L-E-assisted sintering. Our findings indicate that the dynamic
coordination between NCs and their surface ligands during the
printing process governs subsequent chemical fusion. Extending
this understanding, we validated the semiconductive properties of
printed PbS NCs and successfully demonstrated fully printed
multilayer photodiodes responsive to 1500 nm irradiation. Device
performance was further enhanced by integrating asymmetric metal
electrodes—fabricated directly via metal NC printing, a strategy
that offers greater design flexibility compared to conventional
post-lithography metallization.
As hybrid electronics increasingly converge toward integrated
sensing, communication, and computing functions within a single
device, the chemical-assisted nanoprinting method presented here
offers a versatile and practical route for the heterogeneous
integration of multi-material, multi-functional devices on both rigid
and flexible substrate platforms.
