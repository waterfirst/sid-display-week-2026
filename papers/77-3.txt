A Two-Stage Optical Design Methodology for an Eyebox-Expanded
3D AR-HUD
Junnan Jin, Jing Zhang, Yuan Yuan, Jiangbo Yao, Zhifu Li, Bin Zhao
TCL China Star Optoelectronics Display Technology Co. Ltd, Shenzhen, Guangdong, China
Abstract
This paper presents a systematic and efficient two-stage optical
design methodology for developing 3D Augmented Reality HeadUp Displays (AR-HUDs) with an expanded eyebox and
suppressed crosstalk. Conventional design approaches struggle to
balance these competing performance metrics due to their
computational inefficiency and high sensitivity to initial
parameter selection. To overcome these limitations, we introduce
a dual-phase design strategy: first, a computationally efficient
slit-mask model enables rapid parameter space exploration to
identify optimal viewing zone configurations for eyebox
maximization; subsequently, these results are translated into
precise initial parameters for high-fidelity lenticular lens
simulation and optimization in the 3D AR-HUD system. A
prototype developed using this methodology achieves an
expanded horizontal eyebox of ±65 mm while maintaining
crosstalk below 5%, demonstrating excellent agreement with
simulation results. These findings validate the proposed approach
as both feasible and efficient for developing high-performance
3D AR-HUDs, establishing a systematic, replicable technical
framework for future implementations.

1. Introduction
AR-HUDs enhance driving safety and interaction intuitiveness by
projecting critical information as virtual images onto the
windshield, seamlessly integrating with the real-road
environment [1]. The ongoing transition from 2D to 3D display
technology aims to further improve situational awareness through
depth cues, enabling more precise navigation guidance and
collision warnings [2].
Among autostereoscopic 3D solutions, the lenticular lens
approach has emerged as a promising candidate for automotive
applications, owing to its compact form factor, high optical
efficiency, mature fabrication processes, and favorable cost
structure [3]. This technology directs light from interleaved leftand right-eye pixel columns into distinct viewing zones, allowing
drivers to perceive stereoscopic imagery without auxiliary
eyewear.

2. Two-Stage Simulation Design Methodology
2.1 Stage 1: Rapid Parametric Exploration Using
Slit-Mask Model
This study employs a slit-mask as a geometrically equivalent
model for the initial optical design of lenticular lenses. Although
lenticular lenses operate on refractive principles to converge light
while slit-masks utilize physical occlusion for ray selection, both
systems share highly similar geometric relationships in viewing
zone formation [5]. This fundamental similarity establishes the
slit-mask as an effective proxy for preliminary design.
The optical system is constructed as follows: light emitted from
the micro-display is modulated by the slit-mask, then sequentially
passes through the secondary mirror, primary mirror, and
windshield, ultimately forming viewing zones at the eyebox plane
(see Fig. 1 for a schematic diagram). To achieve optimal
performance, we first established a comprehensive slit-mask
optical model and enhanced its accuracy by optimizing pixel
emission angles and luminous region characteristics.
Subsequently, we performed parametric verification and
optimization of the key design parameters: slit pitch and its
distance from the virtual display plane.
The primary objective of this stage is to systematically conduct
parametric sweeps, evaluate the theoretical horizontal eyebox size
under different parameter combinations, and identify the
configuration that maximizes the eyebox range. The optimized
parameters from this stage serve as refined initial inputs for the
detailed lenticular lens design in the second stage. be achieved.

Nevertheless, two key performance metrics of lenticular 3D
systems—eyebox (the spatial region where correct stereoscopic
images are visible) and crosstalk (undesired interference between
left- and right-eye channels)—exhibit high sensitivity to lens
parameters such as pitch and focal length [4]. Conventional
design methodologies typically perform direct optimization on
full lenticular models, a computationally intensive process prone
to local optima convergence and strong dependence on initial
parameter guesses. These limitations often result in protracted
development cycles and suboptimal efficiency.

2.2 Stage 2: Precision Design and Fabrication of
Lenticular Lens
Building upon the optimized parameters derived from the firststage slit-mask model, this phase translates these findings into the
physical design space of the lenticular lens. Using paraxial optical
equations, the optimal slit pitch (p) and mask-to-display distance
(d) are converted into initial structural parameters for the
lenticular lens, comprising lens pitch (p), radius of curvature (r),
and sag height (h).

The emission model implemented in LightTools software is
presented in Fig. 2(c), showing a 780 μm separation between the

notation n=1 indicates that the space between the grating and the
emission layer is modeled as a medium with a refractive index of
1.0 (air/vacuum).

A high-fidelity lenticular lens optical model was subsequently
developed and subjected to refined simulations with multiparameter co-optimization. To enhance manufacturing feasibility,
comprehensive tolerance analysis was implemented, ensuring the
robustness of the optical solution under practical production
conditions. This phase culminates in a complete set of welldefined, manufacturable optical parameters for the lenticular lens,
providing reliable foundation for subsequent prototype
development.

The primary advantage of this approach lies in its substantially
improved design efficiency. By employing the slit grating as a
rapid proxy, extensive design space exploration was achieved
with minimal computational resources, effectively avoiding the
local optima traps common in direct lenticular lens optimization.
This methodology establishes a more deterministic and reliable
pathway from concept to high-performance implementation.
A limitation of this study is its primary focus on horizontal
parallax. Future work will extend this methodology to hybrid lens
designs to achieve simultaneous expansion of both horizontal and
vertical eyebox dimensions.

Figure 9 illustrates the designed pixel arrangement architecture.
In this configuration, the lenticular lens array is oriented with a
14°clockwise tilt, with each lens period corresponding to a "redleft, blue-right" subpixel organization. To enhance image quality,
subpixel units exhibiting excessive crosstalk have been
selectively disabled. The simulation domain encompasses
approximately 13 lenticular periods horizontally and 10 pixel
periods vertically, providing comprehensive evaluation of the
structure's optical performance at practical scales.

4. Conclusion
This paper has successfully proposed and validated a novel dualstage optical design methodology for developing lenticular lensbased 3D AR-HUD systems. The implemented approach, which
utilizes a slit-mask proxy for rapid system-level optimization
followed by high-fidelity lenticular refinement, has demonstrated
both computational efficiency and predictive accuracy. A
functional prototype developed using this methodology achieves
an expanded eyebox of ±65 mm with maintained low crosstalk
(<4.8%), confirming its practical utility. The established
framework provides a robust foundation for significantly
accelerating the design cycle and performance optimization of 3D
AR-HUDs, thereby facilitating their broader adoption in nextgeneration vehicles.
