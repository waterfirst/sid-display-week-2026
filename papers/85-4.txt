Accelerated CGH Generation for AR Head-Up Display Based on DeepCGH
and Yolo
Pin-Yin Huang, Ren-Xiang Ou, Hoang Yan Lin*
1

Graduate Institute of Photonics and Optoelectronics, and Department of Electrical Engineering,
National Taiwan University, Taipei , Taiwan.

Abstract
This work presents a prototype augmented reality head-up display
(AR-HUD) framework that integrates YOLO-based vehicle
detection, monocular depth estimation, and phase-only hologram
generation using the DeepCGH model. Driving-scene video is
processed frame by frame, where detected vehicle regions are
converted into binary target patterns for hologram synthesis, and a
monocular depth map is employed to estimate the relative distance
of each bounding box, enabling depth-aware holographic
rendering. The predicted depth information is incorporated into the
propagation model to assign appropriate reconstruction distances
for different targets. The DeepCGH network generates
corresponding phase holograms in real time, forming a
holographic sequence that mirrors both the motion and depth
variation of vehicles in the original video. Optical validation using
an LCoS-based Fourier reconstruction setup confirms temporal
consistency and depth correspondence in the reconstructed
bounding boxes. Performance evaluation shows stable per-frame
operation for both YOLO detection and DeepCGH inference,
although reconstruction quality degrades in complex or densely
populated scenes. The results demonstrate the feasibility of
combining object detection, monocular depth estimation, and
computational holography within a unified AR-HUD pipeline,
while highlighting future improvements in latency reduction, depth
accuracy, and reconstruction fidelity for practical automotive
applications.

Keywords
hologram, real time, DeepCGH, YOLO, MiDaS, AR-HUD.

1. Introduction
Augmented reality head-up displays (AR-HUDs) are increasingly
explored in advanced driver-assistance systems (ADAS) to present
scene-relevant information directly within the driver’s forward
field of view [1]. To support depth-consistent visualization with
minimal distraction, computer-generated holography (CGH)
provides wavefront-based 3D reconstruction suitable for
windshield-based AR.
However, conventional iterative CGH methods such as Gerchberg–
Saxton (GS) require repeated forward–backward propagation for
phase retrieval, leading to iteration-dependent latency that is
incompatible with frame-by-frame dynamic driving scenes [2].
Learning-based CGH addresses this limitation by replacing
iterative optimization with direct neural inference; DeepCGH
embeds differentiable propagation into training and enables fixedcomplexity, fast phase-only hologram prediction [3–4].
For AR-HUD applications, a key challenge is maintaining
consistency across multiple virtual depths. Most existing
holographic pipelines optimize or reconstruct at a single plane,
whereas depth-aware AR rendering requires multi-plane
propagation and structured depth encoding [5–7]. In parallel, recent
vision pipelines combine object detection with monocular depth
estimation (e.g., YOLO with MiDaS) to infer object-wise relative
depth from a single RGB camera in near-real-time settings [8–9].
Yet these perception systems typically stop at geometric

interpretation and are not directly connected to wave-optical
hologram synthesis.
Building on these developments, this work integrates YOLO-based
vehicle detection and MiDaS depth estimation with a multi-plane
DeepCGH framework. Detected vehicle regions are mapped to
discrete propagation planes and encoded into a volumetric target
representation, forming a unified perception-to-wavefront pipeline
for depth-aware AR-HUD holographic visualization.

2. Theory
2.1 Integrated DeepCGH Algorithm
Figure 1 illustrates the overall pipeline. The process begins with
video input, where each frame is analyzed using YOLO for vehicle
detection and MiDaS for monocular depth estimation. Detected
vehicle regions are fused with their estimated depth values and
discretized into five predefined depth intervals. Based on this
mapping, a five-plane volumetric intensity tensor of size (1024,
1024, 5) is constructed and normalized as the input to the hologram
prediction network.
The 5-plane tensor is then fed into a modified DeepCGH model.
The U-Net–based architecture with Space-to-Depth enhancement
learns a nonlinear mapping from multi-depth intensity targets to a
phase-only hologram. During training, angular spectrum method
(ASM) propagation is applied to all five depth planes using fixed
physical parameters (λ = 532 nm, pixel pitch = 9.2 μm, predefined
propagation distances). A multi-plane reconstruction loss is
computed by comparing propagated intensities with ground-truth
depth slices, and gradients are backpropagated through both the
optical propagation model and the neural network.
After convergence, the trained model performs single-pass
inference without iterative optimization. The input tensor (1024,
1024, 5) is directly mapped to a phase-only hologram (1, 1024,
1024) in one forward pass.
Finally, the predicted phase is numerically propagated to the five
depth planes using ASM to generate multi-depth reconstructions.
The outputs include the phase hologram and five reconstructed
intensity distributions (recon_p0–recon_p4), completing the endto-end perception-to-hologram AR-HUD pipeline.

W, P), where each slice corresponds to one reconstruction depth.
The tensor is normalized to ensure numerical stability during

2. Phase Prediction Network
The multi-depth tensor is fed into a U-Net–based network to learn
the mapping from volumetric intensity targets to a phase-only
hologram. The predicted phase encodes the wavefront modulation
required to reconstruct objects at their assigned depths and is
compatible with phase-modulation devices such as SLMs.
3. Multi-Plane Optical Propagation
The predicted phase is combined with a uniform amplitude to form
a complex optical field. Fourier diffraction is applied to propagate
the field to each depth plane, generating reconstructed intensity
distributions. The stacked reconstructions form a multi-plane
volume for depth-resolved evaluation.
4. Reconstruction Loss and Optimization
Reconstructed intensities are compared with the original multidepth targets using a similarity metric. The resulting loss is
backpropagated through the differentiable propagation model and
network, enabling end-to-end optimization of multi-depth phase
encoding.

3. Result & Discussion
Holograms generated by the proposed YOLO–DeepCGH pipeline
were validated using a Fourier optical reconstruction setup. A
reflective LCoS SLM displayed phase-only holograms under 632.8
nm laser illumination, and reconstruction was performed through a
Fourier lens, enabling evaluation of focal clarity and depth
consistency under realistic optical conditions.
To verify the multi-depth model, multiple controlled simulation
scenes containing spatially separated rectangles at different depths
were designed. The corresponding arithmetic depth mappings for
different plane spacings are summarized in Table 1, where each
equal-interval configuration defines the propagation distances of
Planes 0–4. Quantitative evaluation was conducted using the SSIM
structure metric across reconstruction planes, with numerical
results listed in Table 2. For Plane 4, the 5.0 mm configuration
achieves the highest structure value of 0.9746, compared with
0.9714 (2.5 mm), 0.9651 (3.0 mm), and 0.9592 (3.5 mm),
demonstrating superior axial selectivity and reduced inter-plane
interference.
Among the evaluated scenarios, one representative scene was
selected for detailed presentation. The optical projection results
further confirm the numerical analysis: when focused on the near
rectangle, the far rectangle appears defocused, and vice versa,
illustrating clear depth-of-field separation. Compared with smaller
spacings (e.g., 3.5 mm), the 5.0 mm configuration exhibits stronger
inter-plane separation and more distinct focal transitions.
From a physical optics perspective, the predicted phase encodes
propagation-dependent wavefront curvature corresponding to the
distances defined in Table 1. Constructive interference occurs at the
matched depth plane, while mismatched planes produce defocus
blur. The agreement between the SSIM structure values in Table 2
and the optical projection results confirms that the 5.0 mm spacing
provides the most balanced focal sharpness and reliable multi-plane
depth representation.

In Fourier-based phase-only holography, conjugate image
interference is unavoidable due to the symmetry of the Fourier
transform and phase-only modulation of the SLM. To reduce
background complexity, This scene was selected for optical
validation. Although conjugate overlap remains inherent,
evaluation focused on regions minimally affected by superposition.
When the system was focused on Rectangle 1 (Fig. 5), the 5.0 mm
spacing produced the sharpest reconstruction with clear edge
definition, while the distant rectangle appeared defocused.
Conversely, when focusing on the far rectangle (Fig. 6), the 5.0 mm
configuration yielded the clearest distant reconstruction and visible
foreground blur. Compared with smaller spacings (e.g., 3.5 mm),
5.0 mm demonstrated stronger axial selectivity and clearer interplane separation.
From a physical optics perspective, the predicted phase encodes
depth-dependent wavefront curvature. Constructive interference
occurs at the matched propagation plane, while mismatched planes
produce defocus blur. Despite conjugate interference, the
consistent focal dominance of the 5.0 mm configuration in both
near- and far-focus experiments confirms optimal balance between
focal sharpness and depth separation, validating the robustness of
the proposed multi-depth encoding strategy.

4. Discussion
The experimental results confirm that the proposed YOLO–
MiDaS–DeepCGH pipeline enables depth-aware phase-only
hologram generation with consistent temporal behavior. Both
simulation and optical validation demonstrate that a 5.0 mm plane
spacing provides superior focal sharpness and inter-plane depth
discrimination, verifying the correctness of the embedded multidepth propagation model.
However, performance analysis in Tables 3 and 4 reveals that
system-level bottlenecks limit real-time deployment. Although the
DeepCGH inference alone achieves 87.12 FPS, full hologram
generation including wave propagation drops to 13.99 FPS,
indicating that optical propagation dominates computational cost.
In the perception stage, YOLO runs at 66.91 FPS, while MiDaS
operates at 13.33 FPS, reducing the combined speed to 11.12 FPS.
When disk I/O is included, the overall rate further decreases to 3.52
FPS. These results show that depth estimation and data transfer,
rather than neural inference, are the primary constraints on
throughput.
From an optical standpoint, zero-order diffraction and conjugate
image artifacts remain inherent limitations of phase-only Fourier
holography, reducing image contrast. Future improvements should
focus on artifact suppression and a fully GPU-based tensorstreaming architecture to eliminate disk I/O and redundant memory
movement. Lightweight depth models and simplified network
design can further increase throughput while preserving the
perception-to-wavefront framework.

Overall, the proposed YOLO–MiDaS–DeepCGH architecture
demonstrates stable depth-aware phase-only hologram generation
and provides a scalable foundation for real-time multi-depth ARHUD visualization.

5. Conclusions
This study presents an integrated perception-to-hologram multidepth AR-HUD framework combining YOLO detection, MiDaS
depth estimation, and a physics-consistent DeepCGH phase
encoding model within a Fourier-based phase-only SLM system.
Both simulation and optical experiments validate the embedded
multi-plane propagation model. Across all evaluated scenes, SSIM
structure analysis consistently identifies 5.0 mm plane spacing as
the optimal configuration, achieving balanced focal sharpness and
reliable inter-plane depth discrimination.
