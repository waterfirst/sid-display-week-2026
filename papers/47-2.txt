Study of ultra-fine Redistribution Layers based on Hybrid Glass Substrate†
Feng Liu*, Jin Cheng*, Xikun Wang*, Chuncheng Che*, Xiaojuan Wu*, Yue Li*, Xuecheng
Hou*, Yingying Zhao*, Huiying Li *
*BOE Corporation, Beijing, China
Abstract
This paper presents a hybrid structure integrating ultra-fine
redistribution layers (RDLs) with glass substrate. RDLs with 2/2
line/space features were achieved using an embedded process,
providing a wiring density 2.5 times that of conventional
substrates. The structure eliminates the solder interface,
reducing the package profile and lamination steps, thereby
improving cost and yield. In reliability tests, the structure
showed ≤5% resistance variation without peeling or void
formation, supporting AI/HPC application.

Author Keywords
ultra-fine RDLs, hybrid glass substrate, fine-line layer

1. Introduction
In the post-Moore era, as transistor dimensions approach physical
and lithographic limits, advanced packaging technology has
become a central pathway for continued chip performance
enhancement [1-3]. In artificial intelligence and highperformance computing, the increasing demand for high-density
wiring, large-form-factor packages, and low transmission loss is
accelerating the development of advanced packaging
technologies such as 2.5D and 3D integration. The core structure
of 2.5D and 3D packaging involves organic substrates and
interposer
. On one hand, traditional organic substrates, limited by poor
antiwarpage capability and insufficient dielectric properties, are
increasingly unable to meet the requirements of multichiplet
integration and large-form-factor packaging. In addition,
constrained by the filler particle size in organic build-up
materials, traditional laser-drilling processes face challenges in
achieving high-density interconnects (vias < 10 μm) [4]. On the
other hand, although silicon interposers provide high wiring
precision enabling die-to-die interconnection, they suffer from
high cost, thermal mismatch, and large package thickness.
Packaging configurations using interposers also require at least
two assembly and test cycles, resulting in elevated costs. To
address these challenges, this study proposes an asymmetric
packaging structure that monolithically integrates an organic
interposer with a glass substrate and achieves ultra-fine RDL
fabrication through process innovation, offering a promising
solution for next-generation advanced packaging. The glass
substrate, with its excellent dimensional stability, superior
electrical performance (e.g., lower dielectric loss and reduced
signal crosstalk), and stronger antiwarpage capability, is
considered a key material for overcoming current packaging
bottlenecks. The organic interposer provides high-precision
wiring (L/S ≤ 2/2 μm), enhancing wiring density and enabling
one-time assembly of chiplets and the substrate.
This paper aims to develop a prototype of a high-density RDL on
hybrid glass substrate. The prototype adopts an asymmetric
stacked structure of “fine-line layer (FLL) + glass core.” Its
wiring layer configuration can be represented as X+(2/2/3), where
X denotes 1–3 layers of fine-line wiring from the organic

interposer. To clearly describe the core process and performance,
this study focuses on the example of a single FLL to demonstrate
the achievement of ultra-fine L/S and to systematically present
and discuss the corresponding reliability test results.

2. Experiment
To meet the packaging requirements of high-bandwidth memory
and wide I/O interfaces, an integrated FLLs prototype based on a
glass core board was designed, as shown in Figure 1. The
prototype adopts an asymmetric structure: the glass substrate side
contains two metal core layers, three ABF build-up layers, a
solder mask, and a ball grid array on the lower side; the upper side
of the core contains two ABF build-up layers and the FLL, where
the FLL section includes X layers of high-density wiring. The
overall configuration is X+2/2/3 (X=1–3). The glass core
thickness is 0.7 mm, and the thickness of a single FLL is 5–10
μm. To comprehensively evaluate performance, five L/S test
patterns were designed within the FLL: 2/2 μm, 2.5/2.5 μm, 3/3
μm, 4/4 μm, and 5/5 μm, along with microvias of two diameters
(10 μm and 20 μm). The metal wiring thickness was set at 2–3
μm, pad pitch at 40 μm, and copper pad diameter at 25 μm. These
dimensions were selected to ensure efficient pitch transition
between the chip and the glass substrate.

3. Results and Discussion
In traditional organic substrate packaging, the independent use of
ABF and PI build-up layers has been well established. However,
the adhesion and reliability between ABF and PI in hybrid
substrate applications have not been verified. Therefore,
evaluating the bonding strength between the first FLL and the
underlying ABF dielectric layer is critical for ensuring the
reliability of the hybrid structure. A test plan was designed to
assess the adhesion performance of various PI materials on the
ABF surface. In this test method, PI was spin-coated and cured
on a pre-treated ABF substrate, bonded to a test stub using epoxy
resin, and subjected to a mechanical pull test until interface
failure. The failure strength and failure mode (i.e., whether failure
occurred at the ABF/PI interface) were recorded. As shown in
Table 1, the average adhesion strength of six tested PI materials
exceeded 70.1 MPa. For samples 1–5, failure occurred between
the PI and epoxy rather than at the ABF/PI interface, indicating
that the ABF/PI bond strength surpassed the cohesive strength of
the test epoxy (~70 MPa). Sample 6 failed at the glass/ABF
interface, which was attributed to improper ABF lamination or
curing parameters that caused slight surface wrinkling and
reduced adhesion to the glass (55.8 MPa). After subsequent
reliability tests, SEM inspection of all sample edges revealed no
peeling or void between ABF and PI. The pull test and reliability
test results collectively demonstrate excellent interfacial adhesion
between the PI material and ABF, supporting the feasibility of
developing the hybrid glass substrate integrated with FLLs.

of etch A and B, each with high selectivity for PI and HM
materials, respectively. The key parameter governing this scheme
is the Etch Rate Ratio (ERR), defined as the ratio of the etch rate
of the HM material to the etch rate of the PI under identical
conditions. A lower ERR indicates minimal HM consumption
during PI etching, resulting in more consistent window profile.
An ERR greater than 1 may lead to complete HM removal before
the PI is fully etched. This study compared the ERR
characteristics of two inorganic materials and one organic
material, with results summarized in Table 2. Materials 1 and 2
had ERR values close to 1, indicating HM consumption rates
similar to PI, which would not provide stable etching
performance. These materials would also require much higher
HM thickness compared to PI, causing issues such as poor pattern
consistency and increased etching costs. Material 3 exhibited a
very low ERR value of 0.057, demonstrating excellent etch-stop
capability: only 0.57 μm HM was required to etch 10 μm PI.
Based on these results, Inorganic-2 was selected as the HM
material for subsequent PI etching studies.

Traditional organic substrates typically use a semi-additive
process (SAP) for fine-line wiring, which often requires a thick
plating photoresist as a mask. However, excessively thick
photoresist affects light scattering and chemical uniformity
during exposure and development, making it difficult to achieve
fine RDL lines and spaces (≤4/4 μm). In addition, SAP-formed
lines can collapse during subsequent photoresist stripping. To
address these issues, this work proposes a process route
combining a hard mask with embedded wiring: a thin HM layer
is first patterned using relatively thin photoresist, then high-

As shown in Figure 2, the etching process consists of two steps—
HM etching and PI etching—each requiring different primary
etch atmospheres. Therefore, the etch gas ratio is a key factor
affecting microtrench morphology. Figure 3 illustrates the
resulting trench shapes under various etching conditions. After
process optimization, Figure 3a demonstrates that microtrenches
with L/S = 2/2 μm can be stably achieved with clear edges and no
serrated morphology. When the proportion of etch A was low, gas
exchange and reactant replenishment at the trench center were
better than at the edges, resulting in faster etch rates along both
sides and forming pronounced side grooves (Figure 3b).

Increasing the proportion of etch A significantly reduced the side

Photosensitive polyimide (PSPI) enables direct patterning
through exposure and development; however, the resolution of
mainstream commercial PSPI is typically limited to 10 μm, and
the difference between top and bottom via diameters is often
large, thereby constraining via density. Dry etching can overcome
this limitation, enabling smaller vias with steeper sidewalls and
more uniform top/bottom dimensions, which in turn enhances
achievable interconnect density. In this work, the target microvia
depth was 7.5 μm. As shown in Figure 4a, the optimized dryetching process produced well-formed, residue-free 10 μm
microvias, which is difficult to obtain through traditional ABF
substrate laser-drilling methods. The study found that under
conditions with a high proportion of etch B (Figure 4b), the HM
was excessively consumed at a depth of 5 μm, leaving insufficient
protection for the remaining PI layer, preventing completion of
the via etch. Therefore, deep via etching requires reducing the
fraction of etch B. Concurrently, a low proportion of etch A also
caused side grooves, similar to those observed in trench etching.
More critically, PI etching residues accumulated on the HM
surface under gas flow (Figure 4c). Such accumulation
complicates mask removal and may result in static charge buildup
that can trigger arcing, potentially damaging wafers and
equipment [5-6]. By optimizing the etch gas flow rate and finely
adjusting the etch A/B ratio, high-quality deep via etching was
achieved, yielding a depth of 7.5 μm, a sidewall angle greater than
85°, and no considerable side grooves (Figure 4d). Sub-5 μm via
is currently under development.

To characterize the passive properties of the FLLs, test element
groups (TEGs) for resistance, inductance, and S-parameters were
designed. The electrical performance of wiring with different L/S
specifications was measured, and the results are presented in
Figure 5. Insulation resistance tests (Figure 5a) depicted that as
the line width decreased from 5 μm to 2 μm, insulation resistance
decreased from 1.4 ×1014 Ω to 7.7 × 1013 Ω. These values remain
far above the typical 1012 Ω threshold for advanced packaging,
demonstrating that fine-lines continue to provide reliable
insulation performance. For impedance characteristics (Figure
5b), the DC resistance of 2 μm wiring (~70 Ω) was markedly
higher than that of 5 μm wiring (~20 Ω) at 1 GHz, primarily due
to the increased resistivity resulting from the reduced conductor
cross-sectional area.
High-frequency measurements (Figure 5c) showed that in the 10
GHz band, the insertion loss for 2 μm wiring was approximately
6 dB, compared with 2 dB for 5 μm wiring. This difference is
mainly caused by the enhanced skin effect and the potential
increase in radiation loss associated with narrower line widths.
For inductance characteristics (Figure 5d), the inductance of 2 μm
wiring (~3 nH) was slightly lower than that of 5 μm wiring (~4
nH), as the reduced line width lowers magnetic flux coupling,
which benefits phase stability for high-frequency signals. Overall,
the FLLs meets the electrical performance requirements for midto high-frequency packaging applications.
