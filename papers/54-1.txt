Dual View Display Technology for Enhanced Cockpit Usability
Yasuhiro Sugita, Masashi Mayumi, Mikihiro Noma, Ryoh Kikuchi, Yoshinori Ohhitsu,
Kenichiroh Tsuchida,Yasuyuki Togashi, Hiroshi Hasegawa
Sharp Display Technology Corporation, Mie, Japan
Abstract
We developed a Dual View Display for Center Information
Displays (CID), increasing resolution from 74 ppi to 170 ppi for
high-definition images visible from both driver and passenger
sides. A dual-peak backlight greatly reduced crosstalk. We also
integrated a touch system with User ID, enabling accurate and
personalized operation from either position. This innovation is
expected to improve cockpit usability.

Dual View Display, CID, high resolution, dual-peak

3.1 Panel High-Resolution Enhancement
1. Objective and Background
Sharp marketed Dual View Displays for automotive applications
from 2005 to 2019, with screen sizes ranging from 7.0 inches to
10.3 inches, and these displays were successfully implemented in
production vehicles. In recent years, the growing popularity of
electric vehicles (EVs) and the increasing digitalization of cockpits
have led to a rise in the number of required displays, resulting in
limited installation space. Consequently, the demand for Dual
View Display technology -which enables two separate images to
be displayed within the space of a single display -has increased.
The Dual View Display developed by our company for CID
(Center Information Display) applications significantly increased
resolution from the previous 74 ppi to 170 ppi, allowing two
distinct high-definition images to be viewed from two directions
(driver and passenger). Furthermore, by adopting a two-peak
backlight instead of a conventional single-peak backlight, we
achieved a significant reduction in crosstalk. Since CID
applications require touch functionality for both driver and
passenger screens, we developed a touch panel system equipped
with operator identification capability, named “Dual View Touch.”
This paper reports on the development and performance of this
system.

2. Panel Structure of Dual View Display
The basic principle of the Dual View Display is simple. By forming
a barrier layer with vertical black slits at twice the horizontal pixel
pitch on the surface of the LCD panel, image separation using
parallax becomes possible. With this structure, the driver can view
only image A, while the passenger can view only image B. This
means that two different images can be displayed simultaneously
on a single screen, significantly enhancing the efficiency of
information presentation within limited display space.
Furthermore, the developed prototype incorporates an in-cell
touch panel display to enable touch interaction and adopts a twopeak backlight specifically designed for Dual View Display to
improve visibility. (Figure. 1).

The first Dual View Display panel commercialized by Sharp in
2005 was a 7.0-inch model with a base resolution of 148 ppi. When
a barrier composed of vertical slits was added, the effective
resolution for Dual View Display was reduced by half to 74 ppi. As
a result, the displayed images were of low resolution, and the
vertical barrier lines became visible upon close observation,
leading to degraded display quality.
In the newly developed panel, an 11.8-inch base panel with a
resolution of 340 ppi was adopted, achieving an effective resolution
of 170 ppi for Dual View Display. This improvement eliminates
the visibility of vertical barriers and enables the display of highly

3.2 Crosstalk Reduction
The most critical issue in Dual View Display systems is crosstalk,
where images from the left and right overlap. When crosstalk
occurs, the image intended to be black appears faintly transparent
with content from the opposite view, resulting in a significant
degradation of display quality.

crosstalk in Dual View Displays. Figure 2 shows a comparison of
transmittance when both screens (A and B) are ON and when
screen B is ON while screen A is OFF. As shown in Figure 2, the
transmittance characteristics of Dual View Display are designed for
automotive use, where the viewing angles are fixed at
approximately ±40° based on seat positions. Therefore, both

As a first step in addressing this issue, we examined the origin of
transmittance leakage. Figure 3 shows a microscopic photograph of
a single pixel (B+G+R) located directly beneath one barrier in an
11.8-inch panel, illuminated while all other pixels were off. Light
emitted from the pixel beneath the barrier, which should normally
be invisible, was observed from the front due to diffraction. This
diffraction allows light to spread into angles that should be blocked
by the black barrier, causing the B image to mix into the A image
and appear as crosstalk to the A-side observer.

increases with higher display resolution, their impact can be
expected to become more significant. (Figure 4)

We utilized the fixed viewing angle characteristic of automotive
applications and adopted a two-peak backlight structure, as shown
in Figure 5. This design minimizes light emission toward the front
and concentrates light toward ±40°, reducing crosstalk
significantly. Figure 5 compares the angular luminance
characteristics of a conventional single-peak backlight and the
newly developed two-peak backlight for Dual View Display.
The two-peak backlight is designed to achieve maximum
luminance at ±40°, corresponding to the driver and passenger
viewing positions. Display panels are designed for automotive
applications, where the viewing positions of the driver and
passenger are fixed by seat location, and the observation angle is
approximately ±40°. Therefore, both screens (A and B) are
designed to have peak transmittance around ±40° when active.

4.1
IR ToF (Time-of-Flight) Operator Identification
System
This section describes the operator identification method using
multi-zone IR (Infrared) ToF (Time of Flight) sensing. The system
was developed in collaboration with STMicroelectronics,
combining a ToF sensor (VL53L7CX) with the Dual View Display.
The sensor can measure distances within a diagonal field of view
(DFOV) of 90° using a single unit. Furthermore, its multi-zone
detection capability allows the measurement area to be divided into
up to 64 blocks for distance detection.The sensing system uses
infrared (IR) light at 940 nm and measures distance through
differential processing. With this method, highly accurate distance
measurement up to 65 cm is possible even under strong ambient
light conditions in automotive environments.

4 Touch Panel and Operator Identification
The Dual View Display used in CID (Center Information Display)
system is designed so that the driver and passenger can view
different images simultaneously. Accordingly, individual touch
operations corresponding to each displayed content are required.
However, while the Dual View Display can separate video signals
for left and right views, it traditionally employs a single touch
panel, making it impossible to distinguish whether the input
originated from the driver or the passenger side.

In this study, we propose a method for operator identification on an
in-vehicle Dual View Display (CID) using two Time-of-Flight
(ToF) sensors positioned around the periphery of the display. This
configuration enables pre-detection of the direction from which the
operator's fingers or arms approach, allowing the identification of
the operator. Specifically, the operator is determined by combining
(i) the detection area of the sensor blocks and (ii) the distance
information. Since the seating positions in the vehicle cabin are
fixed, the directions from which operators interact with the CID are
limited, enabling rapid identification through a simple processing
approach (Figure 9).

To address this limitation, we developed the “Dual View Touch
System,” which incorporates a touch panel equipped with an
operator identification function, enabling input separation based on
the user’s position (Figure. 7).
Two methods were developed for operator identification. In this
section, we describe the differences between these two approaches.

Figure 9. Diagram showing two IR ToF sensors measuring
the distance to an object( finger)
This method does not require complex image processing, such as
camera-based facial recognition, and can achieve detection over a
wide field of view (approximately HFOV: 120°). Therefore, it is
highly compatible with Dual View displays and can realize
operator identification with low power consumption and low
latency, compared to conventional complex recognition systems.

The pre-detection functionality is defined as Proximity Detection.
The proximity detection information is immediately transmitted to
the HOST, which uses the received data to determine the target

screen and control processing priority, thereby achieving lowlatency and efficient system response.
Additionally, the proximity detection information is transmitted to
the touch panel IC (TDDI). During touch input, the TDDI
associates the proximity detection information with the touch input,
generating operator identification information (User ID). The
TDDI provides this User ID along with the touch input data to the
HOST, which then controls the screen operation corresponding to
the identified operator, ensuring that only the intended screen is
manipulated (Figure. 10).
However, this method has a limitation: when multiple users
perform touch operations simultaneously, the detection accuracy of
the IR sensors decreases, making concurrent operation from both
sides impractical. To address this issue, the development of a more
accurate operator identification system was considered.
